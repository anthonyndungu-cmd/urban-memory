Festival Season Is Heating Up — Here's Who's Playing Where

June 18, 2026

Summer festival season is shaping up to be one of the most genre-diverse lineups we've seen in years, and that's exactly the kind of crossover energy Music Time exists to track. Across the next two months, stages from Nairobi to Lagos to Johannesburg are set to host a mix of established headliners and rising acts spanning Afrobeats, amapiano, gengetone, and hip-hop — often on the same bill, which says a lot about where audiences' tastes are heading.

What's particularly interesting this year is the number of collaborative sets being announced. Rather than strictly genre-siloed stages, several festivals have leaned into pairing artists from different scenes for one-off live performances, giving fans a reason to show up even if only one name on the poster is familiar to them. We've seen this work especially well at smaller regional festivals where the crowd is more willing to experiment.

For fans trying to plan their summer around specific artists, our Performances page is the best place to check exact dates and venues as they're confirmed — lineups have a habit of shifting in the weeks leading up to an event, so treat early announcements as a starting point rather than the final word.

Why Amapiano's Influence Keeps Spreading Beyond South Africa

June 14, 2026

A few years ago, amapiano was largely understood as a regional South African sound — rooted in deep house, kwaito, and jazz, built around log drum basslines and a slower, more spacious groove than typical club music. That's no longer an accurate description of where the genre sits today.

Producers across East and West Africa have spent the last couple of years absorbing amapiano's rhythmic DNA and folding it into their own local sounds, creating hybrid tracks that don't fit neatly into any single genre box. The result has been a wave of collaborations and remixes that blur the line between amapiano, afrobeats, and even gengetone — and audiences seem to be responding well to the cross-pollination rather than treating it as genre dilution.

This matters for how we think about music scenes generally. The old model of genres developing in isolation, then occasionally borrowing from each other, doesn't really describe how sound moves anymore. Producers are online, listening to everything, and influence spreads in weeks rather than years. Amapiano's expansion is one of the clearest recent examples of that speed.

We expect this trend to keep accelerating through the back half of the year, and we'll be keeping an eye on which producers and artists are leading that fusion.

Behind the Scenes: How Touring Has Changed for Independent Artists

June 9, 2026

Touring used to be something only artists with label backing and tour-management infrastructure could realistically pull off. That's shifted significantly over the past few years, and it's worth talking about because it's changed who gets to build a live audience.

Independent artists today are increasingly booking their own regional tours by working directly with venues, using social media to gauge demand city by city before committing to a date, and relying on local promoters rather than full-scale agencies. It's a leaner model — fewer dates, smaller venues, tighter budgets — but it's allowed artists without major label support to build genuine touring careers on their own terms.

The trade-off is real, though. Without label resources, artists are absorbing more financial risk themselves, and a poorly attended date can hurt more when there's no larger machine behind you to absorb the loss. That's part of why we've seen more artists turn to crowdfunded pre-sales or merchandise bundling to de-risk smaller tours before committing to them.

For fans, this shift means more opportunities to catch artists in smaller, more intimate venues — but it also means tour dates can be added or dropped quickly depending on ticket demand, so it's worth checking our Performances page regularly rather than assuming an announced date is locked in months in advance.

The Rise of Genre-Blending Playlists and What It Means for Artists

June 4, 2026

Streaming platform playlists have quietly become one of the most powerful forces shaping how listeners discover new music, and the genre-blending playlist in particular has changed how artists think about their own sound. A few years ago, an artist working primarily in gengetone might have had limited crossover potential outside that specific audience. Today, the right playlist placement can put that same artist in front of listeners who came in through hip-hop or afrobeats and never would have searched for gengetone directly.

This has had a noticeable effect on how some artists approach production. There's a growing tendency to build tracks with broader sonic appeal — incorporating elements that algorithmically and emotionally read well across genre lines — without necessarily abandoning the core identity of their scene. It's a delicate balance, and not every artist navigates it successfully; some fans have voiced frustration that certain artists are "smoothing out" the rougher, more distinctive edges of their original sound to chase wider playlist placement.

Whether that's a fair criticism probably depends on the artist and the track. What's clear is that playlists have become a genuine pathway to crossover success, alongside (and sometimes instead of) traditional radio or label push. We'll continue tracking which artists are navigating this well versus which feel like they're losing what made them interesting in the first place.

Catching Up: What's Been Dominating Our Listening This Month

May 30, 2026

Rather than a single news item, this post is more of a roundup — a few things across genres that have stood out to us recently and are worth a closer listen if you've missed them.

On the afrobeats side, there's been a noticeable shift toward slower, more melodic production this month, a departure from the high-tempo dancefloor sound that's dominated for the past couple of years. It's not a wholesale genre shift, but enough tracks have leaned this direction that it feels like more than coincidence.

Gengetone, meanwhile, continues to surprise us with its lyrical inventiveness — artists in this space have a particular talent for turning everyday Nairobi life into something rhythmically compelling, often with a wit that doesn't always translate if you're not following the local context closely. It's one of the genres we find ourselves recommending most to newcomers, precisely because it rewards close listening.

And on the hip-hop front, we've noticed more regional artists experimenting with flows that borrow cadence patterns from amapiano and afrobeats, rather than sticking to more traditional boom-bap or trap structures. It's an interesting hybrid, and one we expect to keep showing up more as the year goes on.
